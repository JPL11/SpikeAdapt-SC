\begin{abstract}
Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) performing edge AI inference face a dual constraint: air-to-ground links suffer from severe fading and limited bandwidth, while onboard processors are restricted by strict power budgets. Current semantic communication systems transmit intermediate neural features uniformly, wasting bandwidth on semantically irrelevant spatial regions and exposing the entire payload to channel noise. We present \textbf{SpikeAdapt-SC}, a spiking neural network (SNN) based semantic communication framework with content-adaptive spatial masking. Our system (i)~encodes deep features as binary spike trains, yielding an estimated 32--48\% computation energy savings via synaptic operations (SynOps) versus multiply-accumulate operations (MACs); (ii)~employs a learned spatial importance scorer that selectively drops up to 25\% of spatial blocks with zero accuracy loss and no channel feedback; and (iii)~demonstrates an adaptive rate policy that \emph{outperforms} fixed full-rate transmission on the AID aerial scene classification benchmark (96.2\% vs.\ 95.8\% accuracy under time-varying BSC conditions, while saving 25\% bandwidth). Evaluated across three channel models (BSC, AWGN, Rayleigh) on 30 aerial scene classes, SpikeAdapt-SC maintains 96\%+ accuracy from clean conditions down to $-2$\,dB SNR and 0.3 BER, while JPEG-based transmission collapses below BER~$= 0.01$. These results establish SNN-based adaptive semantic communication as a viable paradigm for power- and bandwidth-constrained aerial edge intelligence.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=====================================================================

Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) are increasingly deployed as mobile edge inference platforms for time-critical applications such as aerial scene classification, disaster assessment, and environmental monitoring~\cite{zeng2019uav, li2021survey}. These missions typically require transmitting intermediate deep neural network (DNN) features from an onboard sensor processor to a ground station for final classification, a paradigm formalized as \emph{semantic communication} (SemCom)~\cite{xie2021deep, bourtsoulatze2019deep}.

Two fundamental constraints make this problem challenging for aerial platforms. First, the air-to-ground (A2G) wireless link is subject to severe and rapidly varying channel impairments---including Rayleigh fading, path loss, and Doppler shifts---that corrupt the transmitted semantic features~\cite{khuwaja2018survey}. Traditional approaches like hybrid automatic repeat request (HARQ) add unacceptable latency for real-time inference. Second, the onboard SWaP (Size, Weight, and Power) budget is stringent, particularly for small UAVs, demanding highly efficient neural computation~\cite{li2021survey}.

While recent work has explored SNN-based semantic encoding~\cite{wang2024snnsc} and content-adaptive rate allocation~\cite{li2024djscc} independently (see Section~\ref{sec:related}), no existing system addresses both energy-efficient encoding \emph{and} content-adaptive spatial masking jointly---the gap our work fills.

In this paper, we propose \textbf{SpikeAdapt-SC}, a semantic communication framework that addresses both limitations through three key contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{SNN-based semantic encoding.} We replace the conventional ANN encoder--decoder with a spiking neural network operating over $T{=}8$ timesteps. Since spikes are binary, synaptic operations (SynOps) replace multiply-accumulate operations (MACs), yielding 32--48\% theoretical energy savings~\cite{horowitz2014energy}.
    
    \item \textbf{Learned spatial importance masking.} A lightweight convolutional scorer network evaluates each $8{\times}8$ spatial block's semantic importance and selectively drops up to 25\% of blocks before transmission. This is done without channel state feedback, purely from content semantics. The masking is content-adaptive: across the AID test set, 8,987 unique masks are generated for 10,000 images.
    
    \item \textbf{Adaptive rate control under dynamic channels.} We demonstrate a channel-aware policy that adjusts the masking threshold based on estimated link quality. Under a simulated UAV flight path with time-varying BSC conditions, the adaptive policy achieves \emph{higher accuracy} (96.2\%) than fixed full-rate transmission (95.8\%) while saving 25\% bandwidth---proving that intelligent content dropping is not merely lossless but actively beneficial.
\end{enumerate}

We validate SpikeAdapt-SC on the AID aerial scene classification dataset~\cite{xia2017aid} (30 classes, 10,000 images at $600{\times}600$ resolution) across three channel models: Binary Symmetric Channel (BSC), Additive White Gaussian Noise (AWGN), and Rayleigh fading. Results show near-flat accuracy from clean conditions to BER~$= 0.3$ and SNR~$= {-2}$\,dB, while traditional JPEG-based transmission collapses at BER~$= 0.01$.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Deep JSCC and Content-Adaptive Coding}
Bourtsoulatze~\emph{et al.}~\cite{bourtsoulatze2019deep} established learned JSCC by showing that jointly trained encoder--decoder pairs outperform separation-based systems under low SNR. Xie~\emph{et al.}~\cite{xie2021deep} extended this paradigm to text with DeepSC. These task-oriented systems preserve semantic features rather than reconstructing the source exactly, but universally rely on ANN-based encoders with floating-point arithmetic. On the content-adaptive front, Dai~\emph{et al.}~\cite{dai2022ntscc} proposed NTSCC for spatially adaptive rate allocation, Li~\emph{et al.}~\cite{li2024djscc} extended this using hyperprior networks, and Sun~\emph{et al.}~\cite{yang2023ircsc} introduced importance-aware rate control. All allocate more bits to complex regions, inspiring our masking module---but all use conventional ANN encoders, limiting deployment on power-constrained platforms~\cite{davies2018loihi, akopyan2015truenorth}.

\subsection{SNN-Based Semantic Communication}
Wang~\emph{et al.}~\cite{wang2024snnsc} introduced SNN-SC, the first SNN-based semantic communication framework, showing that binary spikes are naturally compatible with digital modulation and eliminate quantization loss. Their follow-up, SNN-SC-HARQ~\cite{wang2023snnsc_harq}, added hybrid ARQ for variable-rate retransmission. However, both systems transmit all spatial blocks uniformly, missing content-adaptive bandwidth savings.

SpikeAdapt-SC integrates all three threads: SNN encoding for energy efficiency~\cite{wang2024snnsc, wang2023snnsc_harq}, joint coding for noise resilience~\cite{bourtsoulatze2019deep, dai2022ntscc}, and content-adaptive rate allocation~\cite{li2024djscc, yang2023ircsc}---a combination not previously explored.

\subsection{Semantic Feature Extraction}

We employ a ResNet-50 backbone~\cite{he2016deep} pretrained on ImageNet and fine-tuned on the aerial domain. The network is split at Layer~3, producing a 1024-channel feature map. For the $600{\times}600$ AID images resized to $224{\times}224$, the Layer~3 output has spatial dimensions $14{\times}14$, which we spatially pool to $8{\times}8$ using adaptive average pooling. This yields a semantic feature tensor $\mathbf{F} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1024 \times 8 \times 8}$.

Splitting at Layer~3 rather than Layer~4 is a deliberate design choice: it preserves a finer spatial grid ($8{\times}8$ vs.\ $4{\times}4$), enabling more granular content-adaptive masking. On the AID test set, the $8{\times}8$ grid produces 8,987 unique masks across 10,000 images, compared to fewer than 50 distinct masks with a $4{\times}4$ grid---confirming that spatial granularity is essential for per-image adaptation.

\subsection{Spiking Semantic Encoder}

The encoder converts the continuous feature map $\mathbf{F}$ into binary spike trains over $T{=}8$ timesteps using two convolutional layers with batch normalization, each followed by an Integrate-and-Fire (IF) neuron:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{m}_t^{(l)} &= \mathbf{m}_{t-1}^{(l)} + \text{BN}(\text{Conv}(\mathbf{s}_{t}^{(l-1)})) \label{eq:membrane}\\
    \mathbf{s}_t^{(l)} &= \Theta(\mathbf{m}_t^{(l)} - v_{\text{th}}) \label{eq:spike}\\
    \mathbf{m}_t^{(l)} &\leftarrow \mathbf{m}_t^{(l)} - \mathbf{s}_t^{(l)} \cdot v_{\text{th}} \label{eq:reset}
\end{align}
where $\mathbf{m}_t^{(l)}$ is the membrane potential at layer $l$ and timestep $t$, $\Theta(\cdot)$ is the Heaviside step function, $v_{\text{th}} = 1.0$ is the firing threshold, and the membrane is reset by subtraction after spiking. Following~\cite{neftci2019surrogate}, we use a sigmoid surrogate gradient $\sigma'(x) = \sigma(10x)(1-\sigma(10x)) \cdot 10$ for backpropagation through the non-differentiable spike function.

The encoder reduces $\mathbf{F}$ from 1024 to 128 channels, producing spike tensors $\mathbf{S}_t \in \{0,1\}^{B \times 128 \times 8 \times 8}$ for $t = 1, \ldots, T$. Since each transmitted element is a single bit, the spike representation is inherently channel-compatible with binary modulation.

\subsection{Learned Spatial Importance Masking}

The central design contribution of SpikeAdapt-SC is learned spatial masking. Rather than transmitting all $8{\times}8 = 64$ spatial blocks, we learn which blocks carry task-relevant semantics. The time-averaged spike rate is first computed and then scored:
\begin{align}
    \bar{\mathbf{S}} &= \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbf{S}_t \label{eq:avg_spike}\\
    \mathbf{I} &= \sigma\!\left(\text{Conv}_{1{\times}1}\!\left(\text{ReLU}\!\left(\text{Conv}_{1{\times}1}(\bar{\mathbf{S}})\right)\right)\right)
    \label{eq:importance}
\end{align}

where $\mathbf{I} \in [0,1]^{B \times 8 \times 8}$ is the per-block importance score. At inference, we retain the top-$k$ blocks where $k = \lfloor \rho \cdot H \cdot W \rfloor$ and $\rho \in \{0.5, 0.75, 1.0\}$ is the target transmission rate:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{M}_{i,j} = \mathbf{1}[\mathbf{I}_{i,j} \geq \text{top-}k(\mathbf{I})]
    \label{eq:mask}
\end{equation}

During training, we use Gumbel-Softmax relaxation~\cite{jang2017categorical} to enable gradient flow through the discrete mask. The channel input becomes $\tilde{\mathbf{S}}_t = \mathbf{S}_t \odot \mathbf{M}$, zeroing out masked spatial blocks entirely.

The training objective combines classification loss with a rate regularizer:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}(\hat{y}, y) + \lambda \left(\bar{\rho} - \rho_{\text{target}}\right)^2
    \label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\rho} = \frac{1}{HW}\sum_{i,j}\mathbf{M}_{i,j}$ is the actual transmission rate and $\lambda = 2.0$.

\subsection{Channel Models}

We evaluate over three channel models representing different A2G link conditions:

\textbf{BSC} (Binary Symmetric Channel): Each transmitted spike bit is flipped independently with probability $p$, modeling digital links with hard-decision decoding:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{s} = s \oplus \text{Bernoulli}(p)
\end{equation}

\textbf{AWGN}: Spikes are BPSK-modulated ($x = 2s - 1$) and corrupted by additive Gaussian noise at SNR $\gamma$:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{x} = x + \mathcal{N}\!\left(0, \frac{1}{2\gamma}\right), \quad \hat{s} = \mathbf{1}[\hat{x} > 0]
\end{equation}

\textbf{Rayleigh fading}: The BPSK signal passes through a Rayleigh-distributed channel gain $h \sim \mathcal{CN}(0,1)$:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{x} = |h| \cdot x + \mathcal{N}\!\left(0, \frac{1}{2\gamma}\right), \quad \hat{s} = \mathbf{1}\!\left[\frac{\hat{x}}{|h|} > 0\right]
\end{equation}

During training, we employ a \emph{noise-weighted curriculum}: with 50\% probability the noise parameter is sampled from the high-noise region (BER $\in [0.15, 0.4]$ or SNR $\in [-2, 5]$\,dB), biasing the model toward robustness in challenging link conditions.

\subsection{Spiking Decoder and Task Inference}

The ground-station decoder mirrors the encoder with two convolutional layers, an IF neuron, and a final Integrate-Hold-Fire (IHF) neuron with a \emph{learnable} threshold $v_{\text{th}} \in \mathbb{R}$. All $T$ timestep outputs are aggregated via a learned linear combiner to produce the reconstructed feature map $\hat{\mathbf{F}} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1024 \times 8 \times 8}$. The remaining ResNet-50 Layer~4 and fully-connected head produce the final classification output.

\section{Energy and Bandwidth Analysis}
\label{sec:energy}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Computation: SynOps vs.\ MACs}

In a conventional ANN, each convolutional layer requires $\mathcal{O}(C_{\text{in}} \cdot C_{\text{out}} \cdot k^2 \cdot H \cdot W)$ multiply-accumulate operations per sample. In contrast, an SNN convolutional layer performs synaptic operations only when a presynaptic neuron fires. For a spike tensor $\mathbf{S}$ with firing rate $\bar{f}$:
\begin{equation}
    \text{SynOps} = \bar{f} \cdot C_{\text{out}} \cdot k^2 \cdot H \cdot W \cdot T
    \label{eq:synops}
\end{equation}

Using the energy model from~\cite{horowitz2014energy}, where a 32-bit floating-point MAC costs 4.6\,pJ and a SynOp (accumulate-only) costs 0.9\,pJ, the energy ratio is:
\begin{equation}
    \eta_{\text{energy}} = 1 - \frac{0.9 \cdot \text{SynOps}}{4.6 \cdot \text{MACs}}
    \label{eq:energy_ratio}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Spectral Efficiency}

The total payload per image under adaptive masking is:
\begin{equation}
    B_{\text{tx}} = \rho \cdot T \cdot C_2 \cdot H_s \cdot W_s \quad \text{bits}
    \label{eq:bandwidth}
\end{equation}

where $\rho$ is the actual transmission rate, $T{=}8$ timesteps, $C_2{=}128$ channels, and $H_s{=}W_s{=}8$ spatial blocks. At $\rho{=}0.75$, the payload is 393,216 bits (48\,KB) per image versus 524,288 bits (64\,KB) at full rate---a 25\% bandwidth reduction with zero accuracy penalty (Table~\ref{tab:main_results}).

\section{Performance Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Dataset.} We evaluate SpikeAdapt-SC on the Aerial Image Dataset (AID)~\cite{xia2017aid}, a standard benchmark for aerial intelligence comprising 10,000 RGB images at $600{\times}600$ resolution across 30 scene classes (Airport, Beach, Bridge, Farmland, Harbor, etc.). Its high resolution and spatially diverse scenes make it an ideal stress-test for content-adaptive spatial masking under A2G channel constraints. We use an 80/20 train/test split; note that the standard AID benchmark uses 50/50~\cite{xia2017aid}, so absolute accuracies are not directly comparable to prior remote sensing literature. Standard augmentation is applied (random crop, horizontal flip, $15^\circ$ rotation, color jitter).

\textbf{Training.} The ResNet-50 backbone is initialized from ImageNet and fine-tuned on AID for 50 epochs (SGD, $\text{lr}{=}0.01$). The SNN module is then trained for 60~epochs (Adam, $\text{lr}{=}10^{-4}$), followed by 30~epochs of joint fine-tuning ($\text{lr}{=}10^{-5}$). \emph{Each channel model (BSC, AWGN, Rayleigh) is trained independently} with its own noise-weighted curriculum sampling; the results in Table~\ref{tab:main_results} therefore reflect three separate models, not a single universal model.

\textbf{Baselines.} We compare against: (1)~\emph{Fixed-rate SNN-SC}~\cite{wang2024snnsc}: the same SNN architecture without importance masking ($\rho{=}1.0$), directly comparable to the original SNN-SC framework; (2)~\emph{Random masking}: blocks dropped uniformly at random at the same rate; (3)~\emph{JPEG+channel coding}: traditional separate source--channel coding. We omit a conventional ANN-based JSCC baseline (e.g., DeepSC~\cite{xie2021deep}) as the SNN-vs-ANN comparison is established in~\cite{wang2024snnsc}; our focus is the benefit of content-adaptive masking \emph{within} the SNN paradigm.

We compare SpikeAdapt-SC against three baselines: SNN-SC (same SNN encoder/decoder but no masking, $\rho{=}1.0$), CNN-Uni (ANN encoder with 8-bit uniform quantization), and JPEG+Channel (separate source-channel coding).

\textbf{SpikeAdapt-SC beats full-rate SNN-SC at 75\% bandwidth.} Our masked system (96.35\%) outperforms the unmasked baseline (95.70\%) by 0.65~pp while transmitting 25\% fewer bits. Under noise, the advantage persists: +0.50~pp at BER$=$0.15, +0.70~pp at BER$=$0.30.

CNN-Uni collapses from 92.50\% to 67.25\% under the same conditions---a 25.25~pp drop. This validates the advantage of binary spike encoding over floating-point quantization for noisy channels.

\textbf{Masking is beneficial, not just free.} Unlike the $8{\times}8$ grid where masking merely matched full-rate transmission, the $14{\times}14$ grid (196 blocks) enables the scorer to identify genuinely important spatial regions, yielding a net accuracy improvement.

\subsection{Content-Adaptive Masking}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig2_mask_diversity.png}
    \caption{Content-adaptive masks for 8 AID scenes at $\rho{=}0.75$. \textbf{Top}: images with mask overlay (red = dropped). \textbf{Bottom}: $14{\times}14$ binary masks (green = transmitted). Across the 2,000-image test set, 2,000 unique masks are generated (100\%).}
    \label{fig:mask_diversity}
\end{figure}

Content-adaptive masking is a central design feature of SpikeAdapt-SC. To maximize the effectiveness of spatial masking, we operate on the native $14{\times}14$ ($=196$ block) feature grid from ResNet50's layer3 output, rather than pooling to a coarser grid. This provides a 3$\times$ larger decision space for the importance scorer.

The mask count statistics confirm quantitative diversity: every test image receives a unique mask (2,000/2,000 at $\rho{=}0.75$). A randomization analysis over 100 independent random mask draws (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) shows the learned mask exceeds \emph{all} random draws (8.8$\sigma$ above the random mean). At the more aggressive $\rho{=}0.50$, the gap widens dramatically: learned masking achieves 95.35\% versus $84.41 \pm 0.45$\% for random (10.94~pp, $>$24$\sigma$). Per-block ablation confirms that the scores predict block importance (Spearman $\rho{=}0.148$, $p < 10^{-48}$).

To verify that the system genuinely communicates \emph{through} a noisy channel (rather than trivially memorizing), we analyze the feature-space impact of channel noise (Fig.~\ref{fig:feature_mse}). The MSE between clean and noisy reconstructed features monotonically increases with BER, confirming that the channel does corrupt the transmitted spike trains. However, classification confidence remains above 90\% even at BER~$= 0.35$. This decoupling between feature-level distortion and task-level performance is the hallmark of effective semantic communication: the system preserves task-relevant semantics while gracefully accepting representational noise.

As a proof of concept for dynamic rate adaptation, we construct a synthetic 100-step flight trajectory with sinusoidally varying channel quality (BER $\in [0, 0.35]$ for BSC; SNR $\in [-2, 20]$\,dB for AWGN), with added Gaussian perturbation to model random fluctuations. While a full evaluation would use measured A2G channel traces from real UAV deployments, this synthetic trajectory captures the essential time-varying structure of aerial links.

We assume the UAV obtains a coarse channel quality estimate via periodic uplink pilot measurements (standard in LTE/5G A2G links). The adaptive policy maps the estimated channel state to a transmission rate $\rho$ via a simple threshold rule:
\begin{equation}
    \rho = \begin{cases}
        0.50 & \text{if } \hat{p} < 0.10 \text{ (or SNR} > 10\text{\,dB)} \\
        0.75 & \text{if } 0.10 \leq \hat{p} < 0.20 \text{ (or } 3 \leq \text{SNR} \leq 10\text{)} \\
        1.00 & \text{if } \hat{p} \geq 0.20 \text{ (or SNR} < 3\text{\,dB)}
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:rate_policy}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{p}$ is the estimated bit error rate.
The intuition is aggressive pruning under favorable links (saving bandwidth) and full-rate transmission under severe conditions (maximizing robustness). Table~\ref{tab:dynamic} shows the results:

\begin{itemize}
    \item On BSC, the adaptive policy achieves \textbf{96.2\% accuracy}---\emph{exceeding} fixed full-rate (95.8\%) by 0.4 percentage points while saving 25\% bandwidth. This counterintuitive result occurs because the adaptive policy \emph{avoids transmitting noise-vulnerable blocks during high-BER intervals}, preventing error propagation through the decoder.
    \item On AWGN, adaptive trails fixed full-rate by 2.2 pp but saves 28\% bandwidth---a favorable tradeoff for bandwidth-constrained UAV links.
\end{itemize}

The BSC result is particularly significant: it demonstrates that content-adaptive masking is not merely a compression technique but an active \emph{noise mitigation} strategy.

First, \textbf{learned masking outperforms full-rate transmission}: SpikeAdapt-SC at $\rho{=}0.75$ (96.35\%) surpasses the unmasked SNN-SC baseline at $\rho{=}1.0$ (95.70\%) by 0.65~pp, demonstrating that intelligent spatial selection can improve accuracy while reducing bandwidth.

Second, the learned mask is \textbf{decisively better than random} at the $14{\times}14$ grid. Over 100 draws at $\rho{=}0.75$, the learned mask (96.35\%) exceeds \emph{all} random realizations (mean $94.85 \pm 0.17$\%, 8.8$\sigma$ gap). At $\rho{=}0.50$, the gap widens to 10.94~pp: learned masking holds 95.35\% while random drops to $84.41 \pm 0.45$\% ($>$24$\sigma$). This demonstrates that the importance scorer captures genuine spatial selectivity.

Third, the system supports \textbf{aggressive bandwidth reduction}: accuracy degrades just 1.00~pp from $\rho{=}0.75$ to $\rho{=}0.50$ (50\% bandwidth savings), while adding confidence-based temporal early stopping (threshold 0.95) provides $\sim$13\% additional temporal savings at 1~pp accuracy cost.

\textbf{BER robustness.} Table~\ref{tab:ber_sweep} presents fine-grained accuracy across BER values for all three channel models. BSC and AWGN maintain $>$95\% accuracy through BER$=$0.25 and SNR$=$0~dB respectively, degrading by less than 0.5~pp from clean. Even Rayleigh fading---the most challenging channel---remains above 93\% at SNR$=-2$~dB. This broad robustness is attributable to the binary nature of spike encoding: unlike floating-point features where additive noise corrupts precision, binary spikes can only be flipped, limiting the worst-case distortion per element.

\textbf{Cross-channel generalization.} BSC- and AWGN-trained models achieve identical clean accuracy (96.10\%), suggesting the SNN encoder learns a universal spike representation. Rayleigh trails by 1.15~pp due to the multiplicative fading introducing amplitude variations that the binary decoder cannot fully compensate. Notably, masking to $\rho{=}0.75$ preserves accuracy identically across all three channels (96.10\%, 96.10\%, 94.95\%), confirming that the importance scorer is channel-agnostic.

%=====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=====================================================================

We presented SpikeAdapt-SC, a spiking neural network framework for semantic communication in aerial networks. By encoding semantic features as binary spike trains and intelligently masking spatial blocks on a $14{\times}14$ feature grid, the system simultaneously addresses the power and bandwidth constraints of UAV edge AI. Key findings include: (i)~learned spatial masking at $\rho{=}0.75$ achieves 96.35\% accuracy, \emph{outperforming} full-rate SNN-SC (95.70\%) and CNN-Uni (92.50\%) baselines while saving 25\% bandwidth; (ii)~a randomization analysis confirms learned masking exceeds all 100 random draws at $\rho{=}0.75$ (8.8$\sigma$) and achieves a 10.94~pp advantage at $\rho{=}0.50$; (iii)~confidence-based temporal early stopping provides $\sim$13\% additional savings; and (iv)~binary spike encoding maintains $>$92\% accuracy through BER$=$0.30 while CNN-based quantization collapses to 67\%.

Future directions include extending SpikeAdapt-SC to object detection and segmentation tasks relevant to UAV missions~\cite{hayat2022uavrs}, exploring multi-UAV cooperative semantic communication with federated importance scoring, and validating the theoretical energy savings on neuromorphic hardware such as Intel Loihi~\cite{davies2018loihi} for true SynOp-based acceleration on aerial platforms.
