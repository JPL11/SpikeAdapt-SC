¥title{Fine-Grained Spiking Semantic Communication with Spatial Masking for Aerial Scene Classification}

¥author{¥IEEEauthorblockN{Anonymous Author(s)}

¥maketitle

¥begin{abstract}
UAV edge intelligence must cope with both unreliable air-to-ground links and stringent onboard power limits. We present ¥textbf{SpikeAdapt-SC}, a spiking semantic communication system for aerial scene classification that combines a native $14¥times14$ spatial grid, learned block masking, and low-rate spike-domain transmission. The key design choice is to avoid coarse pooling: on the native grid, masking becomes fine-grained enough to improve robustness under noise rather than merely save bandwidth. Over 5‾seeds with full pipeline retraining, SpikeAdapt-SC achieves $94.86{¥pm}0.63$¥% (AID) and $92.40{¥pm}0.27$¥% (RESISC45) clean accuracy at $¥rho{=}0.75$, with only $¥sim$2‾pp degradation at BER$=0.30$. On RESISC45, masking at $¥rho{=}0.75$ significantly improves BER$=0.30$ accuracy over full-rate ($+$1.67‾pp, $p{=}0.044$), while on AID masking is bandwidth-neutral. At matched equivalent BER, BSC, AWGN, and Rayleigh channels produce near-equivalent accuracy ($¥pm$1‾pp) for all methods, while CNN baselines with 8-bit quantization collapse by 28--56‾pp at BER$=0.30$. MPBN reduces the encoder firing rate to 0.167, corresponding to 37.3$¥times$ lower estimated compute energy via SynOps.
¥end{abstract}

%=====================================================================
¥section{Introduction}
¥label{sec:intro}
%=====================================================================

UAVs are increasingly used as mobile edge-inference platforms for scene understanding, disaster response, and environmental monitoring, but aerial semantic transmission is constrained by both unreliable air-to-ground links and strict SWaP limits on the platform itself‾¥cite{zeng2019uav, li2021survey, khuwaja2018survey}. Split inference is attractive for such platforms, yet transmitting intermediate features over a noisy wireless link remains costly and fragile.

Recent semantic communication (SemCom) systems show that learned feature transmission can outperform separation-based designs under adverse channels‾¥cite{xie2021deep, bourtsoulatze2019deep}. In parallel, SNN-based SemCom has shown that binary spikes are naturally compatible with digital links and event-driven hardware‾¥cite{wang2024snnsc, wang2023snnsc_harq}. However, prior SNN systems transmit all spatial locations uniformly, while adaptive-rate JSCC systems rely on ANN encoders and floating-point features‾¥cite{dai2022ntscc, li2024djscc}. The gap we address is a ¥emph{fine-grained spiking} SemCom system that can both save payload and remain robust under heavy bit errors on aerial datasets.

The central empirical finding of this paper is that ¥emph{granularity matters}. Coarse spatial pooling makes many blocks interchangeable; keeping the native $14¥times14$ feature grid exposes a useful 196-block decision space in which learned masking becomes effective. Our contributions are:
¥begin{enumerate}
    ¥item ¥textbf{Fine-grained native-grid masking.} A BER-conditioned importance scorer selects informative spatial blocks on the native $14¥times14$ feature map. On RESISC45, masking at $¥rho{=}0.75$ improves BER$=0.30$ accuracy by $+$1.67‾pp over full-rate ($p{=}0.044$, 5-seed paired $t$-test); on AID, masking saves 25¥% bandwidth with no accuracy loss under noise ($¥Delta{=}-$0.11‾pp, $p{=}0.84$). A controlled ablation confirms that both BER-conditioning and diversity loss are essential---removing either drops BER$=0.30$ accuracy by 20--48‾pp while leaving clean accuracy unchanged. All ablation variants use the identical pipeline; only the BER branch and diversity term are toggled.
    ¥item ¥textbf{Robust spike-domain transmission.} The spiking encoder maintains high task accuracy under severe BSC corruption: over 5‾seeds, accuracy degrades only $¥sim$2‾pp from clean to BER$=0.30$ on AID, while an 8-bit CNN baseline falls to 52.80¥%. At matched equivalent BER, BSC, AWGN, and Rayleigh channels produce near-equivalent accuracy ($¥pm$1‾pp) for all methods.
    ¥item ¥textbf{Low-activity computation.} MPBN reduces the measured firing rate to 0.167, yielding a 37.3$¥times$ SynOps energy advantage over a full-precision MAC realization under the Horowitz model‾¥cite{horowitz2014energy}.
¥end{enumerate}

¥section{Related Work}
¥label{sec:related}
%=====================================================================

¥subsection{Deep JSCC and Content-Adaptive Coding}

Deep joint source-channel coding (JSCC) established the value of task-oriented feature transmission under channel uncertainty‾¥cite{bourtsoulatze2019deep, xie2021deep}. Later work introduced spatially adaptive bit allocation for ANN-based semantic encoders‾¥cite{dai2022ntscc, li2024djscc}. These systems transmit floating-point features at varying precision, achieving graceful degradation but lacking the natural digital compatibility of binary representations.

¥subsection{SNN-Based Semantic Communication}

SNN-based SemCom has shown that binary spikes are naturally compatible with digital links and can exploit event-driven computation‾¥cite{wang2024snnsc, wang2023snnsc_harq}. Wang‾¥emph{et al.}‾¥cite{wang2024snnsc} demonstrated that temporal spike coding provides inherent robustness to channel noise, and extended this with HARQ for selective retransmission‾¥cite{wang2023snnsc_harq}. However, prior SNN systems transmit all spatial locations uniformly. Our method combines content-adaptive masking ¥emph{inside} an SNN split-inference pipeline for aerial scene classification, while operating on a native spatial grid rather than a pooled representation.

¥subsection{Split-Inference Pipeline}

A ResNet-50 front-end‾¥cite{he2016deep} is split at layer3, producing $¥mathbf{F}¥in¥mathbb{R}^{B¥times 1024¥times14¥times14}$. Unlike earlier pooled variants, we retain this native grid to expose 196 candidate spatial blocks. The SNN encoder maps $¥mathbf{F}$ to binary spike tensors $¥mathbf{S}_t¥in¥{0,1¥}^{B¥times36¥times14¥times14}$ over $T=8$ timesteps using two $3{¥times}3$ convolutions ($1024{¥to}256{¥to}36$), each followed by BatchNorm, MPBN‾¥cite{guo2023mpbn}, and a leaky integrate-and-fire (LIF) neuron:
¥begin{align}
    ¥mathbf{m}_t^{(l)} &= ¥beta ¥cdot ¥mathbf{m}_{t-1}^{(l)} + ¥mathrm{Conv}(¥mathbf{s}_t^{(l-1)}) ¥label{eq:membrane}¥¥
    ¥mathbf{m}_t^{(l)} &¥leftarrow ¥mathrm{BN}_t^{(l)}(¥mathbf{m}_t^{(l)}) ¥label{eq:mpbn}¥¥
    ¥mathbf{s}_t^{(l)} &= ¥Theta¥!¥left(¥mathbf{m}_t^{(l)}-v_{¥text{th}}¥right),¥quad
    ¥mathbf{m}_t^{(l)} ¥leftarrow ¥mathbf{m}_t^{(l)}-¥mathbf{s}_t^{(l)}v_{¥text{th}} ¥label{eq:spike}
¥end{align}
where $¥beta$, $v_{¥text{th}}$ are learnable per-channel parameters and $¥Theta$ is the Heaviside function with sigmoid surrogate gradient‾¥cite{neftci2019surrogate}. MPBN‾¥eqref{eq:mpbn} applies timestep-specific BN to the membrane potential ¥emph{before} thresholding, preventing membrane distribution drift across timesteps and reducing firing rate from 0.266 to 0.167.

¥subsection{Noise-Aware Importance Scorer}

The scorer takes the time-averaged spike map $¥bar{¥mathbf{S}}=¥frac{1}{T}¥sum_t ¥mathbf{S}_t$ and the current BER, and produces per-block importance scores $¥mathbf{I}¥in(0,1)^{14¥times14}$. It consists of three sub-networks:

¥textbf{Noise branch.} An MLP maps BER to per-channel reweighting factors:
¥begin{equation}
    ¥mathbf{w}=1+¥tanh(¥mathrm{MLP}_{¥text{noise}}(¥mathrm{BER})) ¥in (0,2)^{36}
¥end{equation}
The $1+¥tanh(¥cdot)$ design ensures identity initialization ($¥mathbf{w}=¥mathbf{1}$ at training start) and forces multiplicative BER-dependent modulation.

¥textbf{Content branch.} The reweighted features $¥tilde{¥mathbf{S}}=¥bar{¥mathbf{S}}¥odot ¥mathbf{w}$ are scored spatially by two $1{¥times}1$ convolutions ($36{¥to}32{¥to}1$).

¥textbf{Spatial bias + sigmoid.} A second MLP maps BER to a global scalar bias $b$, and the final scores are $¥mathbf{I}=¥sigma(¥text{raw¥_scores}+b)$.

At inference, we retain the top-$k$ entries of $¥mathbf{I}$, where $k=¥lfloor ¥rho HW¥rfloor$. During training, Gumbel-sigmoid relaxation‾¥cite{jang2017categorical} with temperature $¥tau{=}0.5$ enables gradient flow through the discrete mask via STE. The total loss is:
¥begin{equation}
¥mathcal{L}=¥mathcal{L}_{¥text{CE}}+¥lambda_{¥text{rate}}(¥bar{¥rho}-¥rho_{¥text{target}})^2+¥lambda_{¥text{div}}¥mathcal{L}_{¥text{div}}
¥end{equation}
where $¥mathcal{L}_{¥text{div}}=-¥|¥mathbf{I}_{¥text{BER}=0}-¥mathbf{I}_{¥text{BER}=0.3}¥|_2^2$ forces the scorer to produce different masks at different noise levels, and $¥lambda_{¥text{div}}{=}0.05$.

¥subsection{SNN Decoder and Feature Reconstruction}

The decoder mirrors the encoder ($36{¥to}256{¥to}1024$) with MPBN and LIF neurons. A gated linear converter fuses all $2T$ decoder outputs (spikes $+$ membrane potentials at each timestep):
¥begin{equation}
    ¥hat{¥mathbf{F}} = ¥sum_{i=1}^{2T} x_i ¥cdot ¥sigma(¥mathbf{W}¥mathbf{x})_i
¥end{equation}
This learns an optimal weighted combination of spike/membrane snapshots to reconstruct features for the ResNet-50 back-end classifier (L4+FC).

¥textbf{Why does SNN encoding survive high BER?} The SNN encoder produces $T{=}8$ binary spike patterns per channel. A bit flip at BER$=0.30$ corrupts one timestep but leaves the other 7 intact, so the decoder's gated linear converter can reconstruct the original feature from the surviving temporal pattern. In contrast, an 8-bit quantized CNN concentrates all information into a single multi-bit representation where MSB corruption causes catastrophic errors.

¥textbf{Why does masking improve accuracy under noise?} The improvement stems from decoder capacity allocation. When all 196 blocks are transmitted under high noise, the decoder's ResNet-50 Layer‾4 (with fixed representational capacity after global average pooling) must process both high-quality task-relevant blocks and low-quality, noise-corrupted blocks that contribute little semantically but inject significant noise. At $¥rho{=}0.625$, the 122 retained blocks have higher average spike density and carry more redundant information, making them inherently more robust to bit flips. Removing the remaining 74 low-importance blocks eliminates a source of decoder confusion, effectively concentrating representational capacity on high-confidence semantic content. This is analogous to structured dropout‾¥cite{ghiasi2018dropblock}: masking acts as inference-time regularization that becomes more beneficial as channel noise increases.

The 5-seed paired $t$-test confirms this direction on RESISC45 ($¥rho{=}0.75$ vs.¥ $¥rho{=}1.0$: $¥Delta{=}+$1.67‾pp, $p{=}0.044$, though $n{=}5$ limits statistical power). On AID, the masking advantage is seed-dependent ($¥Delta{=}-$0.11‾pp, $p{=}0.84$), consistent with the easier 30-class task having more spatial redundancy.

¥subsection{Latency Analysis}

On a single GPU (NVIDIA RTX 3090), the SNN encoder with $T{=}8$ timesteps adds 0.73‾ms per image compared to the single-pass CNN encoder (SNN enc+dec: 0.79‾ms vs.¥ CNN enc+dec: 0.06‾ms). However, the ResNet-50 backbone dominates end-to-end latency, so the full SNN pipeline (1.30‾ms/image) is only 2.3$¥times$ slower than the CNN pipeline (0.57‾ms/image). On neuromorphic hardware such as Intel Loihi‾¥cite{davies2018loihi}, the $T{=}8$ timesteps would execute with sub-microsecond latency via massively parallel event-driven computation, effectively eliminating the SNN overhead entirely.

¥section{Conclusion}
¥label{sec:conclusion}
%=====================================================================

We presented SpikeAdapt-SC, a spiking semantic communication framework for aerial scene classification. Three findings emerge: (i)‾Fine-grained spatial masking on a native $14{¥times}14$ grid can ¥emph{improve} accuracy under noise---on RESISC45, masking at $¥rho{=}0.75$ improves BER$=0.30$ accuracy by $+$1.67‾pp ($p{=}0.044$), while on AID masking saves 25¥% bandwidth with no accuracy loss. A controlled ablation confirms both BER-conditioning and diversity loss are essential; all variants use the identical pipeline. (ii)‾Binary spike encoding provides near-equivalent robustness across channel types: at matched BER, BSC/AWGN/Rayleigh produce accuracy within $¥pm$1‾pp. Five baselines confirm the robustness of binary encoding: CNN-Uni (8-bit) collapses by 39‾pp, MLP-FC by 56‾pp, and JPEG+Conv exhibits a cliff at BER$=0.05$. A matched-payload CNN-1bit achieves comparable noise resilience to SpikeAdapt-SC (AID: $¥Delta{=}1.39$‾pp; R45: $¥Delta{¥approx}0$‾pp), demonstrating that binary encoding---not temporal coding alone---is the primary driver of robustness. The SNN advantage lies in lower variance and energy efficiency (37$¥times$ via SynOps). (iii)‾MPBN reduces the encoder firing rate to 0.167, yielding $37{¥times}$ estimated energy savings via SynOps. Future work includes HARQ-style retransmission, temporal early exit, and neuromorphic hardware validation on Intel Loihi‾¥cite{davies2018loihi}.
