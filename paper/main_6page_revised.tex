\title{Fine-Grained Spiking Semantic Communication with Spatial Masking for Aerial Scene Classification}

\author{\IEEEauthorblockN{Anonymous Author(s)}
\IEEEauthorblockA{Paper ID: XXXX}}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
UAV edge intelligence must cope with both unreliable air-to-ground links and stringent onboard power limits. We present \textbf{SpikeAdapt-SC}, a spiking semantic communication system for aerial scene classification that combines (i)~energy-efficient spiking transmission via MPBN ($37{\times}$ estimated SynOps savings), (ii)~inherently robust binary spike encoding that degrades only $\sim$3~pp at BER$=0.30$ while 8-bit CNN baselines collapse by 28--56~pp, and (iii)~fine-grained spatial masking on a native $14\times14$ grid (196~blocks) that saves 25\% bandwidth and can improve noise robustness. Over 10~seeds with full pipeline retraining, SpikeAdapt-SC achieves $95.02{\pm}0.55$\% (AID) and $92.34{\pm}0.33$\% (RESISC45) clean accuracy at $\rho{=}0.75$. On RESISC45, masking improves BER$=0.30$ accuracy by $+$1.33~pp over full-rate ($p{=}0.005$, 10-seed paired $t$-test; 95\% BCa bootstrap CI $[+0.72, +2.10]$, surviving Bonferroni correction).
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Semantic communication, spiking neural networks, aerial scene classification, spatial masking, energy efficiency, noisy channels
\end{IEEEkeywords}

%=====================================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=====================================================================

UAVs are increasingly used as mobile edge-inference platforms for scene understanding, disaster response, and environmental monitoring, but aerial semantic transmission is constrained by both unreliable air-to-ground links and strict SWaP limits on the platform itself~\cite{zeng2019uav, li2021survey, khuwaja2018survey}. Split inference is attractive for such platforms, yet transmitting intermediate features over a noisy wireless link remains costly and fragile.

Recent semantic communication (SemCom) systems show that learned feature transmission can outperform separation-based designs under adverse channels~\cite{xie2021deep, bourtsoulatze2019deep}. In parallel, SNN-based SemCom has shown that binary spikes are naturally compatible with digital links and event-driven hardware~\cite{wang2024snnsc, wang2023snnsc_harq}. However, prior SNN systems transmit all spatial locations uniformly, while adaptive-rate JSCC systems rely on ANN encoders and floating-point features~\cite{dai2022ntscc, li2024djscc}. The gap we address is a \emph{fine-grained spiking} SemCom system that can both save payload and remain robust under heavy bit errors on aerial datasets.

SpikeAdapt-SC bridges two lines of work that were previously incompatible: SNN-based SemCom systems transmit binary spikes but offer no spatial adaptivity~\cite{wang2024snnsc, wang2023snnsc_harq}, while adaptive-rate JSCC systems allocate bandwidth spatially but rely on continuous features that are catastrophically fragile under bit-level corruption~\cite{dai2022ntscc, li2024djscc}. Achieving \emph{both} binary robustness and adaptive bandwidth in a single pipeline requires a new BER-conditioned scoring and diversity-loss training scheme (Section~\ref{sec:scorer}). Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Energy-efficient spiking transmission.} MPBN reduces the firing rate to 0.167 (measured empirically across full test sets), yielding $37{\times}$ SynOps energy savings on neuromorphic hardware (Horowitz model~\cite{horowitz2014energy}), independent of channel conditions.
    \item \textbf{Inherently robust binary spike encoding.} Over 10~seeds, SNN encoding degrades only $\sim$3~pp at BER$=0.30$ on AID; 8-bit baselines collapse by 28--56~pp. A matched-payload CNN-1bit achieves comparable robustness, confirming binary encoding as the primary driver; the SNN advantage is energy efficiency and lower variance.
    \item \textbf{Fine-grained spatial masking with BER-aware scoring.} A noise-conditioned scorer on the native $14\times14$ grid (196 blocks) saves 25\% bandwidth. On RESISC45, masking at $\rho{=}0.75$ improves BER$=0.30$ accuracy by $+$1.33~pp ($p{=}0.005$, 10-seed; BCa CI $[+0.72, +2.10]$); on AID, masking is bandwidth-neutral ($p{=}0.55$). Coarser grids consistently degrade accuracy (Table~\ref{tab:granularity}). Ablation confirms both BER-conditioning and diversity loss are essential.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Deep JSCC and Content-Adaptive Coding}

Deep joint source-channel coding (JSCC) established the value of task-oriented feature transmission under channel uncertainty~\cite{bourtsoulatze2019deep, xie2021deep}. Later work introduced spatially adaptive bit allocation for ANN-based semantic encoders~\cite{dai2022ntscc, li2024djscc}. These systems transmit floating-point features at varying precision, achieving graceful degradation but lacking the natural digital compatibility of binary representations.

\subsection{SNN-Based Semantic Communication}

SNN-based SemCom has shown that binary spikes are naturally compatible with digital links and can exploit event-driven computation~\cite{wang2024snnsc, wang2023snnsc_harq}. Wang~\emph{et al.}~\cite{wang2024snnsc} demonstrated that temporal spike coding provides inherent robustness to channel noise, and extended this with HARQ for selective retransmission~\cite{wang2023snnsc_harq}. However, prior SNN systems transmit all spatial locations uniformly. Our method combines content-adaptive masking \emph{inside} an SNN split-inference pipeline for aerial scene classification, while operating on a native spatial grid rather than a pooled representation.

\subsection{Split-Inference Pipeline}

A ResNet-50 front-end~\cite{he2016deep} is split at layer3, producing $\mathbf{F}\in\mathbb{R}^{B\times 1024\times14\times14}$. Unlike earlier pooled variants, we retain this native grid to expose 196 candidate spatial blocks. The SNN encoder maps $\mathbf{F}$ to binary spike tensors $\mathbf{S}_t\in\{0,1\}^{B\times36\times14\times14}$ over $T=8$ timesteps using two $3{\times}3$ convolutions ($1024{\to}256{\to}36$), each followed by BatchNorm, MPBN~\cite{guo2023mpbn}, and a leaky integrate-and-fire (LIF) neuron:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{m}_t^{(l)} &= \beta \cdot \mathbf{m}_{t-1}^{(l)} + \mathrm{Conv}(\mathbf{s}_t^{(l-1)}) \label{eq:membrane}\\
    \mathbf{m}_t^{(l)} &\leftarrow \mathrm{BN}_t^{(l)}(\mathbf{m}_t^{(l)}) \label{eq:mpbn}\\
    \mathbf{s}_t^{(l)} &= \Theta\!\left(\mathbf{m}_t^{(l)}-v_{\text{th}}\right),\quad
    \mathbf{m}_t^{(l)} \leftarrow \mathbf{m}_t^{(l)}-\mathbf{s}_t^{(l)}v_{\text{th}} \label{eq:spike}
\end{align}
where $\beta$, $v_{\text{th}}$ are learnable per-channel parameters and $\Theta$ is the Heaviside function with sigmoid surrogate gradient~\cite{neftci2019surrogate}. MPBN~\eqref{eq:mpbn} applies timestep-specific BN to the membrane potential \emph{before} thresholding, preventing membrane distribution drift across timesteps and reducing firing rate from 0.266 to 0.167.

\subsection{Noise-Aware Importance Scorer}

The scorer takes the time-averaged spike map $\bar{\mathbf{S}}=\frac{1}{T}\sum_t \mathbf{S}_t$ and the current BER, and produces per-block importance scores $\mathbf{I}\in(0,1)^{14\times14}$. It consists of three sub-networks:

\textbf{Noise branch.} An MLP maps BER to per-channel reweighting factors:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{w}=1+\tanh(\mathrm{MLP}_{\text{noise}}(\mathrm{BER})) \in (0,2)^{36}
\end{equation}
The $1+\tanh(\cdot)$ design ensures identity initialization ($\mathbf{w}=\mathbf{1}$ at training start) and forces multiplicative BER-dependent modulation.

\textbf{Content branch.} The reweighted features $\tilde{\mathbf{S}}=\bar{\mathbf{S}}\odot \mathbf{w}$ are scored spatially by two $1{\times}1$ convolutions ($36{\to}32{\to}1$).

\textbf{Spatial bias + sigmoid.} A second MLP maps BER to a global scalar bias $b$, and the final scores are $\mathbf{I}=\sigma(\text{raw\_scores}+b)$.

At inference, we retain the top-$k$ entries of $\mathbf{I}$, where $k=\lfloor \rho HW\rfloor$. During training, Gumbel/Concrete binary relaxation~\cite{jang2017categorical, maddison2017concrete} with temperature $\tau{=}0.5$ enables gradient flow through the discrete mask via STE. The total loss is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}=\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}+\lambda_{\text{rate}}(\bar{\rho}-\rho_{\text{target}})^2+\lambda_{\text{div}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{div}}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{div}}=-\|\mathbf{I}_{\text{BER}=0}-\mathbf{I}_{\text{BER}=0.3}\|_2^2$ forces the scorer to produce different masks at different noise levels, and $\lambda_{\text{div}}{=}0.05$. Although $\mathcal{L}_{\text{div}}$ is applied only at BER endpoints, the MLP-based BER conditioning produces smooth interpolation at intermediate values, confirming continuous mask adaptation rather than two discrete modes.

\subsection{SNN Decoder and Feature Reconstruction}

The decoder mirrors the encoder ($36{\to}256{\to}1024$) with MPBN and LIF neurons. A gated linear converter fuses all $2T$ decoder outputs (spikes $+$ membrane potentials at each timestep):
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathbf{F}} = \sum_{i=1}^{2T} x_i \cdot \sigma(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{x})_i
\end{equation}
This learns an optimal weighted combination of spike/membrane snapshots to reconstruct features for the ResNet-50 back-end classifier (L4+FC).

Table~\ref{tab:main_results} shows four key outcomes. First, the spiking pipeline remains robust: SpikeAdapt-SC drops only 3~pp from clean to BER$=0.30$ on AID, while CNN-Uni collapses by 39~pp and MLP-FC by 56~pp. JPEG+Conv suffers a catastrophic cliff effect at BER$=0.05$, because even a few residual errors corrupt JPEG headers---this cliff is characteristic of separate source-channel coding and motivates joint source-channel approaches. Second, a matched-payload non-spiking 1-bit CNN (CNN-1bit, 10 seeds) achieves comparable noise robustness on AID ($90.86{\pm}1.75$\% vs.\ $92.15{\pm}2.87$\%, $\Delta{=}1.29$~pp), confirming that binary encoding itself provides substantial protection. On RESISC45, the gap is small ($85.77{\pm}2.00$ for CNN-1bit vs.\ $86.18{\pm}4.78$ for SpikeAdapt-SC, $\Delta{=}0.41$~pp). Third, a continuous-valued JSCC baseline (inspired by~\cite{li2024djscc}, 10 seeds) achieves the highest clean accuracy ($96.05$\%), but \emph{collapses} to $22.98$\% on AID and $12.43$\% on RESISC45 at BSC BER$=0.30$---continuous symbols are catastrophically vulnerable to bit flips. Under AWGN, this JSCC baseline retains $95.29$\% on AID even at SNR$=-8$~dB, confirming that its failure is specific to bit-level corruption rather than channel noise in general. Critically, adding adaptive spatial masking to the JSCC baseline (JSCC-Adaptive, $\rho{=}0.75$, 10 seeds) does not rescue it: BSC BER$=0.30$ accuracy remains at $30.65$\% (AID) and $21.34$\% (RESISC45), confirming that the failure mode is inherent to continuous-valued encoding, not a lack of spatial adaptivity. Fourth, spatial masking at $\rho{=}0.75$ saves 25\% bandwidth: on RESISC45, masking \emph{improves} BER$=0.30$ accuracy by $+$1.33~pp over full-rate ($p{=}0.005$, $n{=}10$; 95\% BCa bootstrap CI $[+0.72, +2.10]$, surviving Bonferroni correction), while on AID masking is bandwidth-neutral ($\Delta{=}+$0.26~pp, $p{=}0.55$).

\textbf{Variance caveat.} At BER$=0.30$, per-seed accuracy swings are large (RESISC45 $\rho{=}0.75$: range 76--92\%, std 4.78~pp; AID $\rho{=}1.0$: range 83--95\%, std 3.69~pp). Clean accuracy is stable (std $\leq$0.55~pp); Pearson correlation between clean and noisy accuracy is weak-to-moderate and not significant ($r{=}0.29$--$0.55$, $p{>}0.05$), indicating that noisy-channel robustness depends largely independently of clean-channel model quality.

\textbf{Why does binary SNN encoding survive high BER?} The primary driver of robustness is binary encoding itself: since each transmitted value is 0 or 1, a bit flip changes the value by at most 1 unit, bounding the distortion per symbol. The CNN-1bit baseline confirms this---it achieves comparable noise resilience to SpikeAdapt-SC (Table~\ref{tab:main_results}). A secondary stabilizing factor in the SNN is temporal redundancy: the encoder produces $T{=}8$ spike patterns per channel, so a bit flip at BER$=0.30$ corrupts one timestep but leaves the other 7 intact, providing a modest variance reduction compared to the single-pass CNN-1bit. In contrast, an 8-bit quantized CNN concentrates all information into a multi-bit representation where MSB corruption causes catastrophic errors.

Table~\ref{tab:ber_sweep} and Fig.~\ref{fig:ber_sweep_baselines} present the full BER sweep from 0.00 to 0.30 on both datasets. SpikeAdapt-SC's noise-aware masking degrades only $\leq$3~pp at BER$=0.30$, while the separately trained SNN-SC (no masking) drops 13~pp on AID and 12~pp on RESISC45---demonstrating the benefit of the adaptive scorer under severe noise. All continuous-valued 8-bit baselines exhibit steep degradation. Note the non-monotonic CNN behavior on RESISC45: CNN-Uni improves from 78.32\% (clean) to 82.68\% (BER$=0.10$) before degrading, because the noise curriculum acts as a dropout-like regularizer at low BER. This regularization benefit diminishes as information loss dominates at higher BER.

\textbf{Why does masking improve accuracy under noise?} We hypothesize this stems from decoder capacity allocation. When all 196 blocks are transmitted under high noise, the decoder must process both high-quality task-relevant blocks and noise-corrupted blocks that contribute little semantically but inject confusion. Removing low-importance blocks concentrates representational capacity on high-confidence content---analogous to structured dropout~\cite{ghiasi2018dropblock}---and this effect grows with channel noise. The 10-seed paired $t$-test confirms a significant improvement on RESISC45 ($\rho{=}0.75$ vs.\ $\rho{=}1.0$: $\Delta{=}+$1.33~pp, $p{=}0.005$, surviving Bonferroni correction). On AID, the masking advantage is bandwidth-neutral ($\Delta{=}+$0.26~pp, $p{=}0.55$), consistent with the easier 30-class task having more spatial redundancy.

This near-equivalence is a well-established result in digital communications: for uncoded binary signaling with hard-decision decoding, the BSC is the equivalent channel model for any additive-noise channel at the same bit error probability~\cite{proakis2008digital}. Since SNN spikes are inherently binary ($\{0,1\}$) and the receiver applies hard-decision thresholding, the effective error pattern depends \emph{only} on the aggregate BER, not the physical noise mechanism. The maximum accuracy deviation across all three channels is $< 0.2$~pp for SpikeAdapt-SC, confirming near-equivalent performance under matched BER with hard-decision binary signaling---a practical advantage, as the system need only be trained on BSC to generalize to other digital channels at the same effective BER.

\subsection{Latency}

On a single GPU (RTX 3090), the SNN encoder adds 0.73~ms per image over a single-pass CNN encoder (full pipeline: 1.30 vs.\ 0.57~ms/image, 2.3$\times$ overhead). The ResNet-50 backbone dominates end-to-end latency. On event-driven neuromorphic hardware such as Intel Loihi~\cite{davies2018loihi}, the $T{=}8$ timesteps could execute in parallel, substantially reducing the effective temporal overhead.

%=====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=====================================================================

We presented SpikeAdapt-SC, a spiking semantic communication framework for aerial scene classification. Four findings emerge: (i)~MPBN reduces the encoder firing rate to 0.167, yielding $37{\times}$ estimated energy savings via SynOps---an architectural advantage independent of channel conditions. (ii)~Binary spike encoding yields near-equivalent performance across BSC/AWGN/Rayleigh at matched BER ($\pm$1~pp), consistent with BSC equivalence for uncoded binary signaling with hard-decision decoding. A matched-payload CNN-1bit (10 seeds) achieves comparable noise resilience on BSC (AID: $\Delta{=}1.29$~pp; R45: $\Delta{=}0.41$~pp), while a continuous JSCC baseline collapses to 23\%/12\% at BER$=0.30$---confirming that binary encoding is essential for BSC robustness. (iii)~Under BEC, the SNN's sparse spikes provide unique immunity ($\leq$0.18~pp drop at ER$=0.30$), while CNN-1bit loses 3.9~pp and JSCC loses 4.0~pp---a clear advantage of spike sparsity over dense binary encoding. (iv)~Fine-grained spatial masking on a native $14{\times}14$ grid saves 25\% bandwidth and can improve accuracy under noise---on RESISC45, masking at $\rho{=}0.75$ improves BER$=0.30$ accuracy by $+$1.33~pp ($p{=}0.005$, 10-seed paired $t$-test; 95\% bootstrap CI~$[+0.72, +2.10]$). Future work includes HARQ-style retransmission, temporal early exit, and neuromorphic hardware deployment.
